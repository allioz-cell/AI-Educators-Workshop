Power Supplies & PC Assembly

Undersize a power supply and the fault only shows up under load, which makes it one of the most misdiagnosed problems in the field. Build a parts list to see how the wattage math works, learn what the 80 Plus badge actually promises, and lock in the safety procedure the exam scores as a sequence.

Size a power supply

Build a parts list and watch the recommended wattage move.

The rule to rememberAdd up the draw of every component, then choose a unit with roughly 30% headroom. Supplies run most efficiently near half load, transient GPU spikes need room, and capacitors lose capacity as they age.

80 Plus efficiency ratings

Higher rating means less energy wasted as heat, not more power available.

RatingEfficiency at 50% load (115 V)What it means in practice
80 Plus (White)80%The entry certification. 500 W to the components draws 625 W from the wall.
Bronze85%The common value choice for office and home builds.
Silver88%Uncommon; usually skipped in favor of Gold.
Gold90%The sweet spot for most builds — noticeably cooler and quieter.
Platinum92%Servers and systems that run continuously.
Titanium94%Data center efficiency, priced accordingly.
Read the question carefullyEfficiency describes waste heat, not capability. A Gold 650 W supply and a Bronze 650 W supply both deliver 650 W; the Gold unit just pulls less from the wall and dumps less heat into the case.

Connectors on a modern supply

Every cable has one job, and the shapes are keyed so that mistakes are hard but not impossible.

ConnectorPinsGoes to
Main ATX power24-pin (20+4)The motherboard
EPS12V CPU power4 or 8-pin (4+4)The header beside the CPU socket — separate from the 24-pin
PCIe power6 or 8-pin (6+2)Graphics cards
12VHPWR / 12V-2x616-pinCurrent high-power graphics cards, up to 600 W on one cable
SATA power15-pinDrives, and some RGB or fan controllers
Molex4-pinLegacy fans, older accessories, some case hardware
Do not improviseNever use daisy-chained adapters to fake a missing GPU connector. Each cable is rated for a specific current, and forcing more through a splitter melts connectors — a documented failure mode on high-wattage cards.
Modular vs fixedA modular supply lets you attach only the cables you need, which improves airflow. Cables are keyed to the manufacturer, so never reuse modular cables from a different brand of supply.

Safety and assembly order

The safety rules and the build order that CompTIA scores as procedure.

Electrostatic discharge

  • Wear an anti-static wrist strap connected to the chassis, and work on an anti-static mat.
  • Transport boards and cards in anti-static bags, never on carpet or in a plastic grocery bag.
  • Unplug the system and hold the power button for several seconds to drain residual charge.
  • Handle cards by the edges and the bracket, never by the contacts or chips.
  • Never open a power supply or a CRT monitor. Their capacitors hold a lethal charge long after unplugging, and a wrist strap makes that worse, not better.

Assembly order that avoids rework

  1. Install the CPU, memory and M.2 drives while the board is out of the case — far easier access.
  2. Mount the I/O shield, then stand the board on the case standoffs.
  3. Install the power supply and route the 24-pin and EPS cables behind the tray.
  4. Connect the front panel header, USB and audio before large cards block the pins.
  5. Install the graphics card and connect its dedicated power cables.
  6. Attach storage and its data cables, then tidy the routing for airflow.
  7. Test outside the case or with the side panel off before final cable management.
Thermal designAirflow works when intake and exhaust are balanced. Front and bottom fans pull cool air in, rear and top fans push hot air out, and a case with slightly more intake than exhaust holds positive pressure, which keeps dust out of the unfiltered gaps.
